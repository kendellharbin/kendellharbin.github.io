HAT

The HAT book was the summative project of my four years as an undergraduate. HAT is a critical inquiry into the practice of collaboration. It developed over the course of a year and took final form as a self published book (many thanks to all its generous benefactors).

As shared art-making became a precipitously more popular focus in the art world, I became relatively curious about why that was. Much to my surprise, other artists and scholars were wondering the same thing, however no one was asking the type of questions that preoccupied me. In short, I wanted to know with whom, why, and how people collaborated when engaged with one another creatively. And so HAT came about. 8 projects are featured in total, they range from quilt making, neighborhood event planning, installation arting, amateur programming, and so on. A few specific endeavors, that for one reason or another were given official ‘art’ status, are even featured elsewhere on this website.

The project revolves around the idea that understanding comes through practice. Part thesis paper, part artist book, wholly collaborative (from funding to production); HAT is surely not peerless, though it does raise questions you might be hard pressed to find answered in such a format elsewhere.

2 Mile, 2 Hour

Fred and I proposed to host a workshop at the PLUG Projects book fair.

"In a 2 hour workshop, participants will create a site-specific booklet, learning everything from pre-production to distribution of the final product. Beginning in the gallery with a basic run down of Risograph printing, students will gain an understanding of page layout, printing possibilities, and content development. After a quick dip in the think tank, we’ll surface elsewhere to collect information with which to fill our booklets. Like brave explorers, we will march into the streets and seek out unseen details. This may take the form of poems, doodles, drawings, rubbings, field notes, etc. When our adventure concludes, we’ll retire to the cozy corners of Plug to ask the inevitable: What do we do with what we’ve done? *All participants will leave with an original book of joint creation.”

Naturally, our Risograph broke the day before, so we had to devise a new innovation. The day turned into an impossible game of zine creation where folks were put into ‘scribe’ and ‘dictator’ duos. The scribe had to move the tools, but could not speak. The dictator could touch no tools, only make demands of what to draw. Fred and I ran around the west bottoms and tagged the pairs, which prompted them to reverse their rolls. I can’t say what happened is what I had in mind, but supposedly working with a handicap stimulates next-level creative solutions.
